RUTH ASAWA: "Citizen of the Universe"

Plain text version just below!

“Art will make people better, more highly skilled in thinking and improving whatever business one goes into, or whatever occupation. It makes a person broader.”
– Ruth Asawa


As a teenager Ruth lived in an American concentration camp due to the forced relocation and incarceration of Japanese-Americans by the US government on the eve of World War II. Despite this, she learned her artistic skills from professional artists there and left in the firm belief that art can be life changing and a positive force for social good.


Ruth strived for a truly equal and unified society. She believed that we are all connected on a larger scale with only man-made categorisation dividing us, and truly saw herself to be a “Citizen of the Universe”.

Camera Angles:


Insert Shot

These are particulary welcome during the editing phase as they offer a smooth transition between shots.

Eye Level Shot

The Camera is placed at the subject's height as if the subject is looking at the lense.

These shots are very common because they appear neutral.

High Angle Position

The Camera is above the subject, looking down.

This makes the object look weak, submissive, or frightened.

Low Angle Position

The camera is placed below the objects 'eyes', looking up at them.

Low angles make objects look dominant, aggressive, or ominous.

Dutch Angle Position

Has the camera leaning sideways, transforming the horizon into a slope.

The changing of horizontal and vertical into diagonal lines creates a dynamic composition.

Over The Shoulder (OTS)

A shot of someone or something taken from the perspective of the shoulder of another person or object.

The back of this person or object frames the image that the camera points towards.

Point of View (POV)

A method of shooting a scene or film that expresses the attitude of the director or writer towards the characters or objects within the scene.

Storyboarding

What is a Storyboard?

  • A storyboard is set of drawings that plan the narrative of a film or performance.

What does a Storyboard do?

  • It forces you to think where you might place the camera for any given shot.
  • It shows how those shots might later be cut together to produce a flowing scene.
  • It’s an effective means of communicating ideas to collaborators.

More Camera Techniques: Camera Movements

Pan

The camera swings on its horizonatal axis, similar to moving your head from side to side.

Often used to introduce a new character or object.

Tilt

Used to show the vertical space often hidden by the landscape framing of films.

Tracking Shot

The Camera is moved to follow the action.

Often the camera is mounted on wheels or a rail track. Skateboards or bikes work too (even use your hand if it's a small distance!)

Crane

A mechanical device is used to 'fly' the camera through space.

A stool or chair can be used to achieve a similar effect (be careful!)

Zoom

Used to get nearer to a point of interest in a scene, or for a disorientating effect.

Pull Focus

The Focus of the camera is “pulled”, changing the point of interest in space. Often used to move between two characters or objects.

(Not easy to achieve on a phone)

It’s said that Alfred Hitchcock considered a film done and dusted once he’d completed his storyboard, and that he saw shooting the film itself as a mere technicality.

While this may read like a folk tale, the reality is that Hitchcock rarely rushed to get his camera out on set to capture spontaneous moments. Instead, he preferred to stage his shots well in advance of production.

It’s remarkable to see how closely his finished scenes aligned with his initial conception.

As you embark on your own storyboard in the next task, remember to think ahead about how you might create these actions in real life after - as Hitchcock did...

The Billboard Test: What's your digital footprint?

By this point you are probably ready to jump into the process of creating your own films, however, there is just one more thing that we need to run through...

Audio file pending

You will be creating digital content that is suited to being posted on the internet's many digital platforms.

Before posting anything online, you should always think about:

  • Who might see it - both now and in the future?
  • What personal information does it give away about you? For example, what school you attend or your home address.
  • What impression does it give of you?

If you wouldn't want it to appear on a roadside billboard for all to see, it's probably not a good idea to share it online!

Filming with a smartphone:

Nowadays there isn't the need for expensive cameras for filmmaking. Instead, most of us have a mobile phone in our pocket 24/7 that is always primed to record our experiences.

You most likey already record with your phone with apps such as TikTok or Instagram... To make our art films look more sophisticated than everyday filming, we are going to refine our videography with a mix of techniques and equiptment that will result in a more professional finish.

Filming equiptment:




Please share the the provided materials responsibly as you go about the next task :)

For the next and final task today you don't need to film every shot on your storyboard, this means that you can let someone else have a go after a couple shots if there is demand for the equiptment. If you find the equiptment is all in use already, you can help the others and they can help you back!

Exchanging ideas while you help each other will also mean you learn more from today's experience.







SMARTPHONE FILMING TECHNIQUES:

  1. Film Horizontally - if you film holding the smartphone vertically you will get black bars on both sides of the image when editing it later :(

  2. Check the background noise (both audio and visual). This could be in the form of someones discussion ruining your audio track, or someone eating a sandwich in the back of your shot.

  3. Put your phone on airplane mode so things don't pop up and make sounds/distract you from filming.

  4. Use some tape to keep your phone in place when in its holder if necessary. WE DON'T WANT SMASHED PHONES TODAY!

  5. Film your scene slightly longer than required to give you more space when it comes to editing later.
