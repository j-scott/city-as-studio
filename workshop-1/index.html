What is a Storyboard?

What does a Storyboard do?

  • It forces you to think where you might place the camera for any given shot.
  • It shows how those shots might later be cut together to produce a flowing scene.
  • It’s an effective means of communicating ideas to collaborators.

It’s said that Alfred Hitchcock considered a film done and dusted once he’d completed his storyboard, and that he saw shooting the film itself as a mere technicality.

While this may read like a folk tale, the reality is that Hitchcock rarely rushed to get his camera out on set to capture spontaneous moments. Instead, he preferred to stage his shots well in advance of production.

It’s remarkable to see how closely his finished scenes aligned with his initial conception.

As you embark on your own storyboard in the next task, remember to think ahead about how you might create these actions in real life after - as Hitchcock did...

Filming with a smartphone:

Nowadays there isn't the need for expensive cameras for filmmaking. Instead, most of us have a mobile phone in our pocket 24/7 that is always primed to record our experiences.

You most likey already record with your phone with apps such as TikTok to Instagram... In order for us to make our art films however, we are going to refine our videography with a mix of techniques and equiptment that will result in a more professional finish.

Filming techniques:

SMARTPHONE FILMING TECHNIQUES

  1. Film Horizontally - if you film holding the smartphone vertically you will get black bars on both sides of the image when editing it in order to make up for the wide screen format.

  2. Check the background noise (audio and visual). This could be in the form as someones discussion ruining your audio track or someone eating a sandwich in the back of your shot

  3. Put your phone on airplane mode so things don't pop up and make sounds/distract from filming.

  4. Use tape to keep your phone in place when in its holder if necessary.

  5. Film your scene longer than is required of the scene to give you more space when it comes to editing later.

Post-production editing



In task 4 you shot the individual sequences of a proposed film and maybe even thought about/completed an accompanying audio track. To transform these into short films we need to use post-production editing.

Post-production can be as complex as you want it to be, from simple edits on your phone to full teams of professionals working on high end software. Here are some free apps/softwares that can help you create a film out of your clips.
